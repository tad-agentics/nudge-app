Nudge

Northstar v4.0 — March 29, 2026

1. The Problem

Mike has 14 things he needs to do this week. He knows what they are. He even wrote some of them down — in Apple Notes, in a text message to himself, in an email he starred and forgot. The problem isn't awareness. It's the moment between looking at the list and doing something on it. In that moment, three invisible failures compound: he can't tell which task matters most (judgment failure), he defaults to whatever feels easiest or most recent instead of what's actually important (framework failure), and the tasks he's anxious about — a difficult phone call, an overdue email, a form he doesn't understand — silently slide to the bottom of the stack day after day (avoidance failure). He doesn't notice the avoidance. He just feels a growing background dread that something important is slipping. By Thursday, the avoided task has become urgent. By Friday, it's a crisis. He handles it in a panic and thinks "why didn't I just do this on Monday?" — but next week, the same pattern repeats. He doesn't lack discipline. He lacks a system that sees his blind spots and intervenes before the slide becomes a crisis. No app he's tried does this. They all assume the hard part is writing the task down. The hard part is doing it — especially the ones he doesn't want to do.

2. Primary User

Mike, 31, account coordinator at a mid-size logistics company in Chicago. His job is a mix of internal coordination and client management — tracking shipments, chasing vendor invoices, keeping three account managers updated. His personal life generates its own task load — apartment lease renewal, car registration, dentist appointment he keeps rescheduling, buying his sister's birthday gift, following up on an insurance claim. He's competent and responsible; people at work rely on him. His self-image is "the person who handles things." When his task list piles up, it doesn't just stress him — it threatens his identity. He feels like he's failing at the thing he's supposed to be good at.

Mike's worst moment is Sunday evening around 9pm. He's mentally scanning the week ahead and realizes he forgot to follow up on three things from last week. The dentist appointment he's been rescheduling for six weeks. The proposal he owes a freelance client. The car registration renewal that has a deadline he can't remember. He opens his Notes app and stares at a flat list. Everything looks equally important. Nothing looks easy. He closes the app and opens Instagram.

His second trigger moment is between meetings on weekdays — 10–15 minute gaps where he has enough energy to do something but not enough time to figure out what. In those gaps, he defaults to checking email (feels productive, usually isn't) instead of doing the thing that would actually move his week forward.

Mike doesn't know what an Eisenhower matrix is. He's never heard of "dependency mapping." But he intuitively understands "this one first because the other things can't happen until it's done" — he just can't generate that reasoning reliably for himself under the weight of 14 competing tasks. He needs the reasoning done for him, delivered in language that sounds like a sharp colleague — not a productivity coach.

Secondary User Segment: ADHD / Neurodivergent Adults

15.5 million US adults have ADHD. In a survey of 1,859 ADHD adults, 56.6% reported struggling with procrastinating important tasks and 31.4% cannot accurately estimate task duration. These are not productivity enthusiasts who need a better tool — they are people whose brains structurally resist the exact skills Nudge replaces: prioritization, time estimation, avoidance recognition, and activation energy management. No major productivity app addresses these needs directly. Motion, Todoist, Reclaim, and Sunsama all assume the user can self-assess priority, estimate duration, and follow through when reminded. Nudge's AI reasoning engine, avoidance detection, step decomposition, and energy-aware scheduling are purpose-built for this audience — even though the product is designed for everyone, it disproportionately serves people who need the most help with executive function.

This segment is not a secondary market — it's a distribution wedge. ADHD communities on Reddit (r/ADHD: 2M+ members), TikTok (#ADHDtok: billions of views), and Twitter are the highest-engagement productivity audiences on the internet. A product that genuinely helps with procrastination and avoidance will generate organic word-of-mouth in these communities at a rate no paid campaign can match. The procrastination type quiz (§7c) is designed to capture this audience.

3. The Solution

Nudge is a PWA (Progressive Web App) that launches at app.nudge.app — no app store download required. Native iOS and Android apps follow in Phase 2. One screen matters. Mike dumps every task into a single inbox — typing or pasting (voice capture ships in Phase 2 native). The AI instantly structures each input into a task with an action type (email, call, browse, book, buy, fill form, decide), a semantic category (health, financial, admin, family, work, social), estimated effort, and deadline if detectable.

Then Nudge does the thing no other app does: it thinks about Mike's tasks the way a smart colleague would.

Every todo app can sort by deadline or let Mike drag tasks into priority order. Nudge doesn't sort — it reasons. The AI reasoning engine looks at Mike's entire task list at once and applies nine mental models simultaneously, the same frameworks a competent chief of staff would use if they could see Mike's full picture:

  • Deadline urgency — "Reply to landlord" is due tomorrow. It moves to the top regardless of everything else.
  • Dependency mapping — "Get deposit refund" can't happen until "Reply to landlord" is done. The AI sees this even though Mike never linked them.
  • Avoidance detection — "Call insurance company" has been skipped 4 times this week. Mike doesn't realize he's avoiding it. The AI does.
  • Cost of delay — "Renew car registration" has no hard deadline today, but the late fee kicks in Friday. Every day Mike waits, the cost goes up.
  • Effort-to-impact ratio — "Submit expense report" takes 5 minutes and gets Mike reimbursed $340. High payoff, low effort. The AI inserts it as a quick win between two hard tasks to build momentum.
  • Energy matching — It's 4pm on a Thursday. Mike has had 3 meetings since lunch. The AI surfaces a low-effort task ("order birthday gift online") instead of the hard one ("research health insurance plans") because Mike's energy is depleted.
  • Commitment escalation — Mike already booked the restaurant for his sister's birthday. "Text the family the details" should happen now — otherwise the prior effort is wasted.
  • Emotional weight — "Buy sister birthday gift" has no hard deadline and scores low on every other model. But it's emotionally important. A pure algorithm would bury it below "research laptops." The AI doesn't.
  • Decision fatigue prevention — Mike just completed three tasks that required judgment (choosing an insurance plan, writing a proposal, picking a gift). The next task should be mechanical. "Renew car registration — straightforward, no decisions needed."

The engine also applies two behavioral rules drawn from modern productivity research:

  • The 2-minute rule — If a task takes 5 minutes or less and nothing urgent is blocking its slot, the AI leans into immediacy: "Confirm dentist appointment — 2 min, just do it now." The friction of deciding whether to do a trivial task costs more than doing it. Don't overthink near-zero-effort tasks.
  • Deep work protection — When the calendar shows a 90+ minute open block with no meetings on either side, the AI assigns ONE high-effort task to that block instead of filling it with quick wins. "Research health insurance plans — you have a clear 2 hours. Good time for the hard one." Long uninterrupted blocks are rare and valuable — quick wins go in the 15-minute gaps between meetings.

The output is not a ranked list with scores. It's a single directive with a one-line rationale: "Reply to landlord — this unblocks your deposit refund. 10 min." The rationale sounds like a reason, not a score explanation. Mike doesn't see "priority 8.3 based on dependency and deadline factors." He sees why this task matters right now, in words that make sense. This is what no competitor offers — not Motion ($550M valuation, zero rationale), not Todoist (47M users, zero reasoning), not Reclaim (acquired by Dropbox, zero mental models). Every competitor schedules or lists tasks. None tells you why this one first.

Here's what makes Nudge different from every other todo app: when the AI reasons about and prioritizes tasks, it also proposes a scheduled day on Mike's calendar. During onboarding, Mike connects Google Calendar (the only provider at PWA launch; Apple Calendar ships with native iOS). Each morning, Nudge reads his existing events, finds open slots, and generates a proposed daily plan: tasks time-blocked around his meetings, with hard tasks in peak energy windows and quick wins filling gaps. Mike previews the plan — a simple daily timeline showing task blocks in Burnt Orange alongside his gray meeting blocks — and taps "Approve" to write it to his calendar. He can drag to adjust any block before approving. Nothing touches his calendar without his permission.

This "AI proposes, you approve" approach is deliberate. Research shows that fully autonomous scheduling (Motion's approach) creates what users call "AI Calendar Anxiety" — schedules reshuffling 10+ times per day, leaving users uncertain what they should be working on. Nudge avoids this with a stability-first scheduling philosophy: once approved, the day's plan is locked. New tasks added mid-day go into a "Coming up" queue and are proposed in tomorrow's plan — they don't cascade through today's schedule. If Mike completes a task early, the slot opens up and the next unscheduled task from the queue is offered for that slot — but only if Mike taps "Fill this slot." The calendar stays stable unless Mike explicitly changes it.

After approval, the calendar becomes the execution trigger. Each calendar event includes the task title, the rationale, and a deep link back to Nudge — so when the calendar notification fires, Mike taps it, Nudge opens with the task as "Do now," and "Start" launches the action. Tasks that live only in a list are suggestions. Tasks on the calendar are commitments.

Every task also has a "Start" button. Tapping it opens the right context: a compose window for email tasks, the browser for URL-based tasks, the phone dialer for calls (on mobile; "Copy number" on desktop). The app is a launchpad — it puts Mike inside the action environment within one tap, then gets out of the way.

When Mike finishes, he taps "Done." The app acknowledges the completion, removes the calendar event, and surfaces the next task. If Mike taps "Skip," the task reschedules to the next available slot, drops one position, and gains a skip count. After three skips, the app changes approach: it surfaces the task with an empathetic escalation ("Day 6 with this one — start with just finding the phone number?") and schedules it into a shorter, lower-friction time block. This avoidance detection system is the core differentiator — Nudge doesn't just organize tasks, it catches the ones silently sliding into crisis.

For multi-step tasks ("Plan sister's birthday party"), when the AI detects complexity, it offers: "This looks like a few steps. Break it down?" If Mike confirms, the AI decomposes into discrete actions, sequences them with dependency links, and schedules each step into separate calendar slots respecting the dependency order. For recurring needs ("Submit expense report every Friday"), the app creates a recurring calendar event and regenerates the task on schedule. The user never manages a system. They dump, the app schedules, they do, they done.

Once a week, Nudge surfaces a 60-second review: tasks completed, average time-to-action, and one behavioral insight ("You complete emails in under an hour but phone calls take an average of 4.3 days — try starting tomorrow with the call to the clinic"). Over time, the app learns Mike's specific avoidance triggers, energy patterns, and completion speeds — using this data to schedule smarter: hard tasks in his peak energy windows, avoided tasks in short blocks with low activation energy, quick wins in the gaps between meetings. The emotional outcome after 30 days: Mike opens his calendar and sees his tasks already time-blocked alongside his meetings — the hard things scheduled early, the easy things filling gaps, and the avoided things given short, approachable slots that make starting feel effortless.

3b. AI Reasoning Engine (Core Intelligence)

Design philosophy: Nudge doesn't need a complete task list to be valuable. It needs to re-rank correctly when the list changes. The user's task list is always incomplete — they'll remember things at 2pm Tuesday, at dinner, in the shower. That's fine. Every time a task is added, the reasoning engine re-evaluates ALL active tasks and produces a new ranked list with updated rationales. A task remembered late gets correctly prioritized against everything already there — including inferring new dependencies ("oh, this new task actually blocks the one I was about to do"). The system is designed for continuous, imperfect input. Not a complete brain dump. Capture is the user's job. Reasoning is Nudge's job.

The reasoning engine is Nudge's core product — the thing that justifies the subscription, earns trust, and creates the moat. It is not a rules engine with templates bolted on. It is an AI that sees all of the user's tasks at once, applies multiple mental models simultaneously, reasons about tradeoffs between competing priorities, and produces a ranked list with a specific, context-aware rationale for each task. The user experiences this as: "The app just knows what I should do first, and the reason always makes sense."

Architecture: Batch Reasoning + Validation Gate

The engine operates in two layers:

  • Layer 1 — AI Reasoning (LLM): Receives ALL active tasks in a single batch call. Applies mental models holistically — seeing cross-task relationships, user behavioral patterns, and calendar context that no per-task rule can see. Outputs a ranked list with rationale and metadata per task.
  • Layer 2 — Validation Gate (deterministic, zero LLM): Enforces hard safety constraints the LLM cannot override. Catches edge cases. Provides template-based fallback for low-confidence outputs. This layer exists to prevent trust-breaking failures, not to make decisions.

Layer 1 — AI Reasoning Call

Trigger events: A reasoning call fires when the task list changes state: new task added (debounced — multiple additions within 2 minutes batch into one call), task completed, task skipped, morning re-rank (daily at user's wake time), calendar change detected (new meeting added/removed). Estimated frequency: ~150–250 calls/month per user.

Layer 2 — Validation Gate

The validation gate is a deterministic post-processing layer. It exists to catch errors the LLM might make and enforce constraints that should never be violated, regardless of what the AI reasons.

Gate RuleWhat It ChecksAction on Failure
Hard Deadline Safety Net Any task with a deadline within 48 hours (deadline_confidence > 0.8) MUST be in the top 3. If the AI ranked it lower, force it to position 1. Override the AI's judgment — missing a hard deadline is catastrophic for trust.
Quick Win Rhythm No more than 3 consecutive tasks with effort > 20 minutes. If the AI ranked 4+ hard tasks in a row, insert the highest-ranked task with effort ≤ 15 min after position 3.
Rationale Confidence Rationale confidence score must be ≥ 0.6. If below 0.6, replace with a safe template fallback: "{{effort}} — good one to tackle next." The rank stays as the AI set it; only the rationale is replaced.
Rationale Length Rationale must be ≤ 15 words. Truncate at the nearest sentence boundary before the 15-word limit. If no clean boundary, fall back to template.
Forbidden Words Rationale must not contain any word from the EDS Forbidden Words list. Replace with template fallback. Log the violation for prompt tuning.
Tone Check Rationale must not contain guilt language ("you still haven't", "overdue", "you need to"), exclamation marks, or emoji. Replace with template fallback. Log for prompt tuning.
Hallucination Guard Rationale must not reference information not present in the task data. If the rationale mentions a person, deadline, or consequence not in the task text or user context, it's hallucinated. Replace with template fallback. This is the most critical gate — a hallucinated rationale ("Call your mom — she's worried") when the user never mentioned their mom will destroy trust permanently.

System Prompt Design Principles

The system prompt for the reasoning call is a critical product artifact. It must be tuned, versioned, and tested. Key principles:

  • Think like a chief of staff, not an optimizer. The AI should reason about what a competent human would do with this information, not solve a mathematical optimization problem. "Mike has a hard call to make and a quick email — do the email first to build momentum, then tackle the call" is chief-of-staff reasoning. "Task A scores 8.3, Task B scores 7.1" is optimizer reasoning.
  • Show your work in the rationale, not in the ranking. The user never sees the ranking logic. They see the rationale. The rationale must feel like a reason, not a score explanation. "Dave's been waiting 3 days and this unblocks the invoice" is a reason. "High priority due to dependency and cost of delay factors" is a score explanation.
  • Err toward safe, defensible recommendations. If two tasks are close in priority, choose the one that's harder to argue against. "Reply to the client who's been waiting" is always defensible. "Research new laptops" before "reply to client" requires a specific, visible justification.
  • Surface avoidance patterns, not individual skips. Saying "you skipped this 4 times" is useful. Saying "you've been avoiding financial tasks all month — this is the easiest one, start here" is transformative. The AI should always look for the pattern behind the skip count.
  • Respect emotional weight without being presumptuous. Family tasks, health tasks, and relationship tasks carry emotional significance. The AI should rank them appropriately — not lower than admin tasks just because they lack deadlines. But the rationale should never presume to know the user's feelings: "This one matters to you" is acceptable; "I know family is important to you" is presumptuous.
  • Never explain the engine. The rationale is the user-facing output. It must never reference models, scoring, algorithms, or the reasoning process. "I used the cost-of-delay model to determine..." is a failure. "The client's been waiting 3 days" is the correct output.
  • Apply the 2-minute rule aggressively. If a task has effort_estimate ≤ 5 minutes, no dependencies blocking it, and no higher-urgency task competing for the slot — the rationale should lean into immediacy: "2 minutes — just do it now" or "5 min and it's off your plate." Don't overthink low-effort tasks. The friction of deciding whether to do a 2-minute task costs more than doing it. This is James Clear / David Allen GTD logic embedded as a behavioral instruction, not a separate model — it's a flavor of Effort-to-Impact that fires when effort is near-zero.
  • Protect deep work blocks in scheduling hints. When the AI sees that the user has a 90+ minute open block with no meetings on either side, the scheduling_hint for the highest-effort task should be deep_work_block — signaling the calendar engine to place ONE hard task in that block rather than filling it with 4 quick wins. The rationale for that task should acknowledge the opportunity: "You have a clear 2 hours — good time for the hard one." Quick wins go in the 15–30 minute gaps between meetings. Long open blocks are rare and valuable — don't waste them on admin. This is Cal Newport's Deep Work principle translated into a scheduling instruction.

RANKING PRINCIPLES — How to make judgment calls.
═══════════════════════════════════════════════════════════

WHEN TWO TASKS ARE CLOSE IN PRIORITY:
- Choose the one that's harder to argue against. "Reply to client
  who's been waiting" is always defensible. "Research laptops"
  before "reply to client" requires visible justification.

WHEN DEADLINE AND AVOIDANCE CONFLICT:
- A task due in 48 hours outranks a task skipped 5 times but with
  no deadline. Deadlines within 48 hours always win.
- A task skipped 5 times WITH a deadline in 7 days outranks a
  non-avoided task with the same deadline. The avoidance makes it
  more likely to become a crisis.

WHEN EFFORT-TO-IMPACT AND ENERGY CONFLICT:
- If the user has high energy (morning, open calendar), surface the
  high-effort/high-impact task — not the quick win.
- If the user has low energy (post-meetings, evening), surface the
  quick win — even if a higher-impact task exists.
- Energy match overrides effort-to-impact when energy is depleted.

WHEN EMOTIONAL WEIGHT HAS NO OTHER SIGNAL:
- A family/health/relationship task with no deadline, no avoidance,
  and no dependency should still rank in the top half of the list —
  never dead last. If all other models say it should be last, move
  it to the midpoint. The user will feel the ranking is "wrong" if
  "buy sister's birthday gift" is below "organize desktop files."

MOMENTUM MANAGEMENT:
- The ideal ranked list alternates between hard and easy tasks:
  hard → quick win → hard → quick win → moderate.
- Never 3+ hard tasks in a row. Never 3+ quick wins in a row.
- If the list is all hard tasks, insert the easiest one at position 3.
- If the list is all easy tasks, surface the most impactful one first.

What Batch Reasoning Enables (vs. Rule-Based Cascade)

The previous architecture used a deterministic cascade — 6 rules applied sequentially per task. The AI reasoning engine replaces this with holistic reasoning across the entire task list. Here's what that unlocks:

  • Cross-task inference. The AI sees "reply to landlord" and "get deposit refund" in the same list and infers the dependency — even if the user never stated it. A cascade can only see dependencies that are explicitly stored in the schema.
  • Pattern-level avoidance detection. The AI sees that 3 financial tasks have been skipped this week, not just that one task has skip_count = 3. It can surface the category-level pattern in its rationale: "Financial tasks are piling up — this is the quickest one." A cascade only evaluates skip counts per task.
  • Context-aware rationales. The AI knows Mike just completed 3 hard tasks and can say "Simple one — no decisions needed" for the next task. A template can only reference the individual task's properties, not the user's recent activity.
  • Adaptive momentum management. The AI can decide that after 2 avoidance-escalated tasks, Mike needs a quick win before another hard one — even if the cascade rules would have ranked another hard task next.
  • Nuanced scheduling hints. The AI outputs scheduling_hint per task, which the calendar engine uses for slot assignment. Instead of a binary "morning = hard, afternoon = easy," the AI reasons: "This specific task is a call, Mike does calls best on Thursday mornings, schedule it there."

3e. Calendar Scheduling Engine

The scheduling engine is the bridge between "what to do" (priority engine) and "when to do it" (the user's actual day). It reads the user's calendar, identifies open slots, and assigns each task a time block. This is what elevates Nudge from a smart list to an execution system.

Calendar Provider Selection

During onboarding (after auth), the user connects their calendar:

  • Google Calendar (Phase 1 — PWA launch) — via Google OAuth. Scopes: calendar.events (read/write events), calendar.readonly (read existing schedule). Works in PWA, iOS, and Android. The only calendar provider at launch.
  • Apple Calendar (Phase 2 — native iOS) — via iOS EventKit framework. Uses native on-device calendar access. No OAuth required — iOS permission prompt only. Works on iOS only. Events sync via iCloud to all Apple devices. Added when native iOS ships.

In Phase 1 (PWA), there is no provider selection screen — Google Calendar is the only option. In Phase 2 (native), Apple Calendar is added as a second option. Nudge connects to one calendar provider at a time — no merging logic.

Scheduling Logic: Preview → Approve → Stable Day

Stability-first philosophy. Motion recalculates hundreds of times daily, creating "AI Calendar Anxiety" — users report 10+ reschedules in a single day. Nudge takes the opposite approach: the AI proposes a plan once each morning. The user previews, adjusts, and approves it. Once approved, the day is locked. Mid-day changes go to tomorrow's plan, not today's. This is Morgen's "AI suggests, you decide" philosophy combined with Reclaim's graduated-defense stability — the hybrid approach that user research shows generates the highest satisfaction.
StepWhat HappensDetails

This is the only scenario where today's approved plan changes without the user initiating it — and even then, it requires user confirmation.

Scheduling Rules

  • Max tasks per day: 5 scheduled blocks (default, adjustable 3–8). This prevents calendar overload and matches the research finding that overloaded schedules trigger avoidance of the entire app.
  • Minimum gap between task blocks: 5 minutes.
  • Unscheduled overflow: Tasks beyond the daily max remain in the "Coming up" queue. Rationale: "Not scheduled yet — your calendar is full today."
  • Look-ahead planning: The morning plan generates today only. Future days are planned each morning, not in advance. This prevents the cascading-reschedule problem that plagues Motion.
  • No-schedule preference: Users can toggle "Don't schedule my tasks" in settings. In this mode, Nudge works as a priority-only "Do next" list with no calendar events. Calendar scheduling is the paid tier's hero feature but not mandatory.
  • Avoidance-aware scheduling: Tasks skipped 3+ times get scheduled in shorter blocks at high-energy times. A 30-min phone call the user keeps avoiding → 10-min block: "Find the insurance company phone number" (decomposed first step) at 9am Tuesday.
  • Recurring task scheduling: Recurring tasks create recurring calendar events. "Submit expense report every Friday" → recurring Friday 9am block.
  • User-moved events: If Mike drags a Nudge event in his calendar app (Google Calendar or Apple Calendar), Nudge respects the new time — the user's calendar is the source of truth for timing once written.
  • Deep work protection: When the scheduling engine finds a 90+ minute open block with no meetings on either side, it assigns ONE high-effort task (the one with scheduling_hint: deep_work_block from the AI reasoning engine) rather than filling it with multiple quick wins. Quick wins go in the 15–30 minute gaps between meetings. Long uninterrupted blocks are rare — don't waste them on admin. If no task has the deep_work_block hint, the engine defaults to the highest-effort task available.

What Nudge Writes to the Calendar

Each Nudge calendar event contains:

  • Title: Task title (e.g., "Reply to landlord about lease")
  • Duration: effort_estimate_minutes
  • Description: Rationale text + "Open in Nudge" deep link
  • Calendar: A dedicated sub-calendar, auto-created on first sync. Default color: Burnt Orange (#D4763C) — warm and distinctive, visually separates task blocks from meetings. User can change color or hide this sub-calendar in their calendar app.
  • Reminders: 5-minute reminder before the scheduled time (uses native calendar notification). This means the user gets a calendar notification even when Nudge isn't open — the calendar becomes the trigger surface.
  • Status: "Free" (not "Busy") — so Nudge tasks don't block the user's availability for real meetings.
Why a sub-calendar, not the main calendar: Mixing Nudge task blocks with real meetings creates visual clutter and makes the user feel over-scheduled. A dedicated sub-calendar lets the user see their Nudge tasks alongside meetings but also toggle them off when sharing their calendar or joining a meeting. The sub-calendar events are marked "Free" so colleagues don't see the user as unavailable during task blocks.

6. Competitive Moat

Moat type: Data moat (proprietary behavioral data) + Switching cost moat (accumulated personalization)

The moat is not prioritization (commoditizing — Apple Intelligence and Gemini will offer basic versions). The moat is the avoidance profile — a per-user behavioral model that no competitor can replicate without months of usage data.

After 90 days, Nudge knows:

  • Which task categories Mike avoids (phone calls: avg 4.3 days to complete vs. emails: avg 0.8 days)
  • When he's most productive (Tuesday and Thursday mornings, 9–11am)
  • How long tasks actually take him — measured completion times per action_type and category
  • Which rationale language motivates him ("quick win" drives 73% completion; "important" drives 31%)
  • His avoidance triggers (tasks in the "financial" category, tasks requiring calls to strangers, tasks with ambiguous next steps)
  • His skip-to-crisis pattern (tasks he skips 4+ times have a 60% chance of becoming urgent within 7 days)
  • His optimal scheduling patterns — which calendar slots have the highest task completion rates, which time blocks he consistently ignores, and how to schedule avoided tasks in the windows where he's most likely to actually do them

A competing app starts from zero. The switching cost isn't data portability — Mike can export his task list anywhere. The switching cost is losing an AI that understands his specific procrastination fingerprint. That fingerprint compounds with every completed and every skipped task.

Moat timeline: Month 1–3: no moat, product wins on UX speed and rationale quality alone. Month 3–6: basic behavioral patterns emerge, recommendations visibly improve, retention strengthens. Month 6+: avoidance profile is deep enough that recommendations feel uncannily personal. Switching becomes psychologically costly. This timeline is honest — the moat doesn't exist at launch.

PWA (Desktop Access)

The PWA is not a separate product — it's the same React codebase with a responsive layout, deployed as an installable web app at app.nudge.app. It exists to check the "desktop access" box that editorial reviewers and power users expect, and to provide a better surface for the morning plan preview/approve flow (the daily timeline is easier to scan on a larger screen).

What the PWA supports:

  • Full task capture (text + paste, no voice — browser mic permissions are unreliable)
  • AI prioritization + rationale ("Do next" view)
  • Morning plan preview + approve (responsive daily timeline)
  • "Start" button (opens email/browser/calendar in new tabs)
  • "Done" / "Skip" actions
  • Weekly review
  • Settings, auth, subscription management
  • Service worker for offline task list (read-only — AI calls require connectivity)

What the PWA does NOT support:

  • Voice capture (ships with native Phase 2 — browser mic APIs are unreliable)
  • Push notifications on iOS Safari (Apple restriction — PWA push works on Android Chrome and desktop browsers only)
  • Apple Calendar via EventKit (native iOS only — PWA users must use Google Calendar for scheduling)
  • Phone dialer "Start" action (no tel: on desktop — falls back to "Copy number")

Build cost: Minimal incremental effort if the mobile app uses React Native with a shared React core. The PWA is a responsive web wrapper around the same components. No separate codebase, no separate team. The primary work is responsive layout for the morning plan timeline and the "Do next" card on wider screens.

PWA is Phase 1 — native is Phase 2. Nudge launches as a PWA at app.nudge.app. Native iOS + Android apps ship in Phase 2, adding voice capture, Apple Calendar, native push, and IAP. The PWA remains available after native launch for desktop access — morning plan approval on a laptop, quick task capture from a desktop browser, and editorial review criteria. The landing page shifts from "Use in your browser" (Phase 1) to "Download on App Store / Play Store" with a secondary "or use in your browser" link (Phase 2).
